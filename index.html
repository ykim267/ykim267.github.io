Welcome! I am currently a Ph.D. candidate in Political Science at the University of California, Merced, where my research explores the underlying factors that drive human behavior and thought. I am particularly interested in uncovering the factors that influence decision-making, such as shared trauma, narratives of victimhood, and religious or dogmatic beliefs. My work spans a comparative analysis—from examining how these factors shape voting behavior within nations to their roles in international issues like land-related disputes.

My dissertation consists of three interrelated papers. The first investigates how land-claim framing influences third-party support in conflicts. The second examines how layered historical traumas—such as colonialism and war—shape political attitudes. The third explores how victimhood narratives become moralized, driving polarization and undermining democratic compromise.

I am a native Korean speaker, and I am proficient in both English and Japanese. Additionally, my experience as an exchange student in Israel has equipped me with intermediate-level skills in Arabic and Hebrew.
